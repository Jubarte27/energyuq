# Title: Uncertainty Quantification for Energy Efficiency Analysis of Scientific Applications at Exascale

## Authors: Matheus Machado, Matheus Costa, Mariana Costa, Arthur Lorenzon, Antigoni Giorgiadou, Bronson Messer

### Long Abstract (max 800 words)

The rapid growth of high-performance computing (HPC) systems has driven substantial increases in energy consumption, making energy efficiency a central concern in the design and operation of modern computing platforms. As systems scale toward larger node counts and higher degrees of parallelism, power and energy constraints increasingly limit achievable performance and system utilization. Beyond infrastructure and environmental costs, energy inefficiency also affects scheduling flexibility, reliability, and the feasibility of long-running scientific simulations. Consequently, there is a growing need for methods that allow developers and system operators to understand how application behavior and system configuration influence energy consumption, rather than focusing exclusively on execution time.

Energy usage in parallel applications depends on a combination of hardware and software factors, including processor and accelerator operating frequencies, thread and process counts, memory access behavior, communication intensity, and runtime policies such as dynamic voltage and frequency scaling. These factors interact in non-trivial ways and often exhibit variability across executions, even under nominally identical conditions. Sources of variability include hardware heterogeneity, contention for shared resources, operating system activity, and differences in application input or execution paths. As a result, energy measurements are subject to fluctuations that cannot be fully explained by deterministic models or single-run experiments.

Most existing approaches to energy-aware optimization rely on average-case measurements or fixed performance models, implicitly assuming that system behavior is stable and repeatable. While such approaches can be effective in controlled environments, they provide limited insight into how sensitive energy consumption is to changes in configuration parameters or to execution-time variability. This limitation becomes more pronounced at scale, where small sources of uncertainty can accumulate and lead to unexpected behavior. As a consequence, parameter choices that appear optimal in isolated experiments may not be reliable when deployed in production settings.

In this work, we propose using uncertainty quantification (UQ) as a systematic approach to analyze energy efficiency in parallel applications. UQ provides statistical techniques for characterizing variability in system inputs and outputs, allowing uncertainty to be explicitly modeled rather than treated as noise. Instead of focusing solely on point estimates of execution time or energy consumption, UQ enables the analysis of distributions, variability, and confidence bounds. While UQ is well established in scientific simulation and modeling, its application to energy efficiency analysis in HPC remains limited.
We present a tool-based methodology that applies UQ to the empirical study of parallel applications running on HPC systems. Our approach is based on controlled experiments in which key system parameters, such as processor frequency, the number of threads or processes, and affinity settings, are systematically varied. For each configuration, we collect repeated measurements of execution time and energy consumption using hardware-supported monitoring interfaces. These measurements are treated as stochastic observations rather than deterministic values, allowing variability to be captured directly in the analysis. Using these data, we construct statistical models that relate configuration parameters to execution time and energy consumption. A central component of our methodology is variance-based sensitivity analysis, which enables us to quantify the contribution of each parameter to the observed variability in energy usage and performance. This analysis enables distinguishing parameters that strongly influence energy consumption from those whose impact is marginal, even though both affect execution time. Such information is particularly useful when trade-offs between performance and energy must be considered explicitly.

To reduce the cost of exploring large configuration spaces, we employ surrogate models that approximate the relationship between system parameters and measured outcomes. These models enable efficient evaluation of untested configurations while preserving the statistical properties observed in the original data. Uncertainty propagation techniques are then used to assess how uncertainty in configuration parameters propagates into derived metrics, such as energy-to-solution or energy–time ratios. An important aspect of our work is the joint analysis of performance and energy consumption. Rather than optimizing one metric independently of the other, we analyze how uncertainty affects their relationship. This allows us to identify configurations that provide favorable energy–performance trade-offs while maintaining predictable behavior across runs. In practice, such configurations may accept small increases in execution time in exchange for consistent and measurable reductions in energy consumption, which is often acceptable in energy-constrained environments. The proposed tool is designed to be independent of specific applications or hardware platforms. It can be applied to a wide range of parallel workloads and system architectures, provided that execution time and energy measurements are available. 

### Short Abstract (max 200 words, 1 paragraph)

Energy efficiency has become a critical constraint in high-performance computing (HPC) as systems scale toward larger node counts. In modern HPC platforms, energy consumption is influenced by complex interactions among hardware and software parameters, including operating frequencies, concurrency levels, memory behavior, and runtime policies. These interactions are further affected by execution-time variability arising from hardware heterogeneity, resource contention, and system noise. As a result, energy measurements often exhibit significant fluctuations that are not captured by deterministic models or single-run experiments, limiting the reliability of traditional energy-aware optimization approaches. This work proposes using uncertainty quantification (UQ) as a systematic method for analyzing energy efficiency in scientific HPC applications. Instead of relying on average-case behavior, we explicitly model the variability in execution time and energy consumption as functions of system configuration parameters. We present a tool-based methodology that combines controlled experimental measurements with variance-based sensitivity analysis, surrogate modeling, and uncertainty propagation. This approach enables the identification of configuration parameters that most strongly affect energy consumption and performance variability, as well as the assessment of energy-performance trade-offs under uncertainty. Moreover, the proposed tool is application-agnostic and applicable across a wide range of scientific HPC workloads and architectures.
